Understanding the cycle so the math makes sense

A menstrual cycle has two main parts. The follicular phase runs from the first day of your period up to ovulation and can vary in length. The luteal phase runs from ovulation to the next period and is relatively steady for most people, usually around 12 to 14 days. That single fact unlocks much of the calendar math. If you know the average length of your cycle, you can estimate ovulation by subtracting your luteal phase length. Many people who ask how to calculate ovulation day from cycle length end up using this principle without realizing it. For example, if your cycle is 30 days and your luteal phase is about 14 days, you likely ovulate around day 16. If your cycle is 26 days, ovulation often lands near day 12 to 14. Ovulation predictor kits, usually called OPKs, detect luteinizing hormone in your urine. Basal body temperature to confirm ovulation happened

Basal body temperature (BBT) offers confirmation rather than prediction. Progesterone after ovulation raises resting temperature by a few tenths of a degree. If you measure your temperature each morning before getting out of bed, you may notice a small sustained rise after ovulation. Common myths that confuse timing

There are two myths that derail many plans. The first is the idea that everyone ovulates on day 14. That only holds for a perfect 28-day cycle and even then itΓÇÖs an average, not a guarantee. If you ask how many days after period is ovulation and you get a universal answer, be skeptical.
